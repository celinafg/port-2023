About KLOA

KLOA is a startup that focuses on making search easier by saving users typing effort. This new search experience consists of an input interface and a contextual search algorithm (see this section for interaction map). Ideally, businesses with mobile shopping apps like Amazon or Instacart would be potential clients of KLOA.

Previous diary studies on KLOA highlighted usability issues that the team is currently iterating on, including what types of feedback to provide users. This research then seeks to understand:

  • 1

    ‍What effect would priming participants have on their experience (i.e. prefacing it as a search experience versus a keyboard)?
  • 2

    How willing participants would be to learn how to use KLOA? How would they prefer to learn about it?
  • 3

    ‍Barriers and frustrations participants encounter when using KLOA in the context of shopping on a mobile app?

KLOA is aware of usability issues and are actively iterating on the design. So our study focused on two hypotheses:

  • Hypothesis 1

    If users don't think of KLOA as a keyboard, they won't try to use it like a QWERTY keyboard
  • Hypothesis 2

    Users will be able to use KLOA if onboarding instructions are provided (a variation of "best case" testing)

What we tested

1 - Latest release

Per our client’s request and to better mimic the newest version of their design, we taped over the search bar and the word ribbon that existed in this iteration.‍‍‍

testing the prototype

2 - Onboarding instructions

To understand users’ initial impression of KLOA and how new mental model can be constructed in a short amount of time, we designed a one-page instruction, introducing the basic functionality of the keyboard.‍‍‍

Test Environment

Where were the studies conducted?

All our sessions were conducted in libraries on campus.The participant and two members (one moderator, one observer) were in the room at each time.The rest of team observed on Zoom.‍‍‍

general setup

There are three components to our setup: camera 1, camera 2, and the prototype. Camera 1 captures participant's hand gesture and movement from above. Camera 2 captures participant's facial reaction and body language. The prototype records the user's interaction with the product.‍‍‍

analysis
and findings

Participants felt lost when they didn't get the feedback they were expecting while typing

3 - Critical

The lack of feedback while typing resulted in participants expressing doubt and re-doing tasks. 4 out of 5 participants mentioned frustration about not receiving typing feedback (by typing feedback, we are referring to seeing “a” in the search input field after tapping on the key). They struggled while typing because only search suggestions were visible. One participant tried to type the letter ‘b’ but kept getting results starting with ‘c’. The participant tried to long press, double tap, and go into all caps mode, then proceeded to restart the query by deleting input six times.

Participants struggled with the visuals of the input interface‍‍‍

2 - Moderate

Most participants struggled with the layout and were unable to understand what the blue keys were and how to use them. They struggled to locate the letters they needed. One participant attempted to search for ‘broccoli’, and was so focused on the keyboard that they didn’t see ‘broccoli’ show up as the top search suggestion. They proceeded to delete/restart the query.‍‍‍

Participants felt frustrated by the experience and preferred QWERTY‍‍‍

3 - Critical

Most participants felt the keyboard created a barrier to mobile shopping and struggled to understand what value it brought. 3 out of 5 participants abandoned KLOA and switched back to QWERTY to complete the task. One participant blamed themselves for being unable to type, and found relief when they found the QWERTY keyboard through the red dot.‍‍‍

final thoughts

What I learned

It was challenging working with a prototype that wasn't fully functional. The NLP algorithm wasn't completely predictable (although it worked for a few words we chose), and the company was actively going through redesign and testing with users at the same time we were conducting our own tests. Even though it wasn't ideal that there was tape on the prototype, I still believe that choosing to test with the tape did not significantly affect the results of our research, and we still obtained valuable insights tthat were actionable for the company.

If I had more time for this project, I would have suggested Rapid Iterative Testing and Evaluation (RITE) with figma designs to avoid any complications in research results that were caused by the algorithm not working properly. Because the product is very early stage, it would be more ideal to adopt this kind of method in order to test more versions of the design which is more important for this kind of product in order to understand mental models around keyboards more deeply.

The research we conducted has shown opportunities for the company to explore - how much information is needed by users to learn this kind of layout, and what kind of medium would be the most universally understandable to communicate this information? How much information needs to be provided for onboarding in order for users to feel confident about using the keyboard? We chose to use a one-pager that was very minimal in text and images just to get a gauge of where the users are at with learning a new layout for search purposes, and to inform future onboarding materials in the future.

All in all, it was a great learning experience and I learned a lot about usability testing with our mixed method approach. This kind of usability testing is more similar to what would happen in the real world, with a prototype that is constantly being updated, that may not be production ready yet.
